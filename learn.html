Physical Design Flow

1. Floorplanning

The first step in defining the chip architecture. It involves determining the size of the chip, placing macros (memory, IP blocks), and defining the power grid (P/G rings and straps).

  • Core and Die area definition
  • Macro placement & port positioning
  • Power Grid (IR drop analysis setup)
💡 Interview Tip: Why avoid placing macros at the center of the chip? Because it can cause routing congestion and IR drop issues later. Always place them along the boundaries first.

Quick Summary

Goal: Fix die size and macro positions to ensure enough space for standard cell placement and routing.

2. Placement

Standard cells of the logic netlist are placed within the available rows in the core area. Placement aims to minimize wire length while satisfying timing and congestion constraints.

💡 Interview Tip: What is 'local congestion'? It occurs when too many pins are in a small local area, leaving no room for routing tracks. Use "Density Control" to fix it.

Key Metrics

  • • WNS (Worst Negative Slack)
  • • TNS (Total Negative Slack)
  • • Cell Density (%)

3. Clock Tree Synthesis (CTS)

Building a tree-like network of buffers and inverters to distribute the clock signal to all sequential elements (flip-flops) with minimum skew and latency.

💡 Interview Tip: Why use H-Tree? To ensure the clock arrives at every flip-flop at the exact same time, minimizing global skew.

CTS Targets

Minimize Skew, Minimize Insertion Delay, and Minimize Clock Power.

4. Routing

Connecting the placed cells and macros using metal layers. Routing happens in two phases: Global Routing (defining tracks) and Detail Routing (defining specific metal layers and vias).

💡 Interview Tip: DRC vs LVS? DRC ensures manufacturing rules are met. LVS ensures the final layout matches the schematic netlist.

Routing Rules

Min Spacing, Min Width, Antenna Effect, Crosstalk Noise.
