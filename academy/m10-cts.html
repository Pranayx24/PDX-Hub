Phase II: Execution

Clock Tree Synthesis

The heartbeat of the silicon. Balancing clock arrival to millions of registers for zero skew and low power.

1. The Goal: Skew, Latency, and Power

Clock Tree Synthesis (CTS) involves building the buffer network that distributes the clock signal from its source (PLL/Pad) to every flip-flop (sink) in the design.

Key Performance Indicators (KPIs):

  • Latency (Insertion Delay): Time from clock source to the furthest sink. High latency leads to massive variation in PVT corners.
  • Skew (Global): The arrival time difference across the whole chip. Local Skew (between logically connected registers) is much more important for meeting timing.
  • Power: The clock tree typically accounts for 40-50% of a chip's total dynamic power. Power reduction is achieved via Integrated Clock Gates (ICG).
Useful Skew (Post-CTS Optimization)

While the goal is Zero Skew, we can intentionally add "Useful Skew" to help a critical data path. By delaying the capture clock by 50ps, we give 50ps more time for the data to travel, fixing negative setup slack without changing the logic.

2. Distribution & NDR Rules

We use specific metal rules for clock nets to ensure signal integrity:

  • H-Tree: Ensuring synchronous arrival through physical symmetry.
  • NDR (Non-Default Rules): Using 2x width and 2x spacing for clock wires to reduce resistance and increase performance.
  • Shielding (VSS-Guarding): Routing ground wires on both sides of a clock net to prevent it from coupling with (and corrupting) nearby sensitive data nets.

Interview focus: Clock Skew

"Is positive skew good or bad for setup timing?"

Answer: Positive skew (where the capture clock arrives after the launch clock) helps setup timing but hurts hold timing. It effectively increases the available clock period for the data to travel.
