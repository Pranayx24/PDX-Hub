Phase II: Execution

Clock Tree Synthesis

The heartbeat of the silicon. Balancing clock arrival to millions of registers for zero skew and low power.

1. The Goal: Skew and Latency

Clock Tree Synthesis (CTS) involves building the buffer network that distributes the clock signal from its source (PLL/Pad) to every flip-flop (sink) in the design.

Key Performance Indicators (KPIs):

  • Latency (Insertion Delay): The time it takes for the clock to travel from source to the furthest sink. Must be minimized for high performance.
  • Skew: The difference in clock arrival times between any two registers. Goal is zero (balanced) skew.
  • Slew (Transition Time): The slope of the clock edge. Too slow a slew leads to poor timing and high power.

2. Distribution Topologies

We use specific structures to balance the clock signal across the die:

  • H-Tree: A recursive "H" structure that ensures equal path length to every leaf. Most common for ASIC.
  • Clock Mesh: A grid of clock wires that provides massive redundancy and extremely low skew but consumes huge power.
  • Fishbone: Used for smaller designs or local distributions.

Interview focus: Clock Skew

"Is positive skew good or bad for setup timing?"

Answer: Positive skew (where the capture clock arrives after the launch clock) helps setup timing but hurts hold timing. It effectively increases the available clock period for the data to travel.
