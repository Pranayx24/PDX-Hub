CMOS & ASIC Design

The masterclass for Physical Design engineers. From semiconductor physics to high-performance ASIC methodology.

1. The CMOS Transistor Basics

CMOS (Complementary Metal-Oxide-Semiconductor) is the technology used to construct integrated circuits. It uses pairs of P-type and N-type MOSFETs (Metal-Oxide-Semiconductor Field-Effect Transistors) to perform logic functions.

Key Insight: The "Complementary" nature means when one transistor type is ON, the other is OFF, resulting in negligible static power dissipation.

Formula: P_total = P_dynamic + P_static. In CMOS, P_static is nearly zero.

2. Silicon Fabrication Flow

ASIC manufacturing involves hundreds of lithography cycles on a silicon wafer. The process includes:

  • Wafer Preparation: Growth of pure silicon ingots sliced into thin wafers.
  • Doping: Adding impurities (Phosphorus for N-type, Boron for P-type) to modify conductivity.
  • Etching: Removing unwanted material to create paths and gate structures.
  • Metallization: Connecting devices with Copper or Aluminum layers.

3. ASIC vs FPGA Methodology

While FPGAs are programmable on the field, ASICs are hardware-hardened at the foundry. The tradeoff involves NRE costs vs Performance.

Full-Custom Design

Designing every transistor and net manually. Used for high-performance processors and analog blocks. Maximizes PPA but high effort.

Semi-Custom (Cell-Based)

Using pre-designed and pre-verified "Standard Cell Libraries". This is the standard path for modular ASIC design.

Interview focus: CMOS Reliability

"Explain the body effect and how it impacts threshold voltage (Vth)."

Answer: The body effect occurs when the source-to-bulk voltage (Vsb) is non-zero, shifting Vth and potentially causing timing failures if not modeled during sign-off.
