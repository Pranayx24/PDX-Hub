Phase III: Advanced

Advanced Timing

Crosstalk & Noise. Deep dive into signal integrity and the impact of wire-to-wire capacitance on silicon timing.

1. Crosstalk Delay (SI)

Signal Integrity (SI) is driven by the coupling capacitance (Cc) between adjacent metal lines. This lead to the **Miller Effect**, where the effective capacitance of a net depends on the switching of its neighbor.

Crosstalk Logic:

  • Miller Factor (1.0 vs 2.0): If the aggressor is static, Cf = Cc. If the aggressor switches in the opposite direction, Cf = 2·Cc, creating a massive delay hit.
  • Timing Windows: Crosstalk only occurs if the Aggressor and Victim transit at the same time. STA tools use "Timing Windows" to filter out noise that happens while the victim's logic is held stable.
  • Crosstalk Glitch: A noise spike that occurs on a net that is supposed to be at a stable logic level. If this glitch passes through a register, it can flip the stored data.

2. Signal Integrity Mitigation & PrimeTime-SI

Solving SI is achieved by reducing the Coupling Capacitance (Cc) relative to the Ground Capacitance (Cg).

  • Upsizing Victim: Making the victim gate stronger to "resist" the charge injection from the aggressor.
  • Spacing: Increasing the distance between nets. Since Cc ∝ 1/d, doubling the space significantly reduces noise.
  • Layer Hopping: Moving one of the two coupled nets to a different metal layer entirely.
  • PrimeTime-SI: The industry-standard sign-off tool that accounts for crosstalk windows and accurately calculates the SI-impact on WNS.

Interview focus: Clock Shielding

"Why is clock shielding required for sub-7nm designs?"

Answer: Clocks switch 100% of the time, making them the most aggressive source of noise. In tight-geometry nodes (sub-7nm), the lateral capacitance is so high that an unshielded clock can easily cause timing failures in neighboring data nets.
