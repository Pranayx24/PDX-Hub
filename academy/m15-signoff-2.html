Phase III: Advanced

Sign-off Reliability

The endurance of silicon. Analyzing EM and IR drop to ensure your chip doesn't burn out over time.

1. Electromigration (EM) Analysis

EM is the transportation of metal atoms caused by current flow. It is modeled by **Black's Equation**, which relates the Mean Time to Failure (MTTF) to current density and temperature.

EM Rule of Thumb:

MTTF ∝ 1 / J² · exp(Ea / kT)

Temperature is the biggest enemy. A 10°C rise in chip temperature can cut the wire's lifespan by 50% due to EM.

Self-Heating Effects (SHE): In FinFET nodes, the 3D fin structure is surrounded by insulating oxide (low thermal conductivity). This traps heat in the channel, significantly exacerbating EM risks compared to flat planar nodes.

2. Dynamic IR Drop & Decap Strategy

Dynamic voltage drop (DvD) occurs at the clock edge. We solve this by placing **Decap Cells** (Decoupling Capacitors) throughout the core.

  • Local Reservoirs: Decaps act as local energy storage. When a gate switches, it pulls current from the decap instead of the distant power strap.
  • Frequency Response: Decaps must be distributed uniformly to handle the GHz-scale transients of modern processors.
  • Power Grid Robustness: Every power segments must meet the threshold of IR drop defined in the chip's power budget (typically < 10% VDD).

Interview focus: EM vs IR Drop

"What is the difference between a Signal EM violation and a Power EM violation?"

Answer: Power EM is caused by steady DC current flowing through VDD/VSS rails. Signal EM is caused by high-frequency AC switching current in local interconnects. Both require wider wires or more vias but are analyzed by different tools.
