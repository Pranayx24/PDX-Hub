Phase III: Advanced

Low Power Design

The art of efficiency. Using UPF, Clock Gating, and Multi-Voltage domains to minimize the power footprint of high-performance silicon.

1. Dynamic and Static Power reduction

Power management is now a mandatory requirement for mobile and server chips alike. We optimize for power across both the architecture (UPF) and the implementation stages.

Techniques Hierarchy:

  • Clock Gating: Disabling the clock to inactive flip-flops. Reduces dynamic power by up to 60%.
  • Power Gating (MTCMOS): Physically cutting off the VDD to a whole block using "Sleep Transistors". Eliminates static leakage.
  • Multi-Voltage (MSV): Running different blocks at different voltages (e.g., 0.8V for core, 1.2V for I/O). Requires Level Shifters.
  • Isolators: Required when an "off" block's output goes to an "on" block. Prevents crowbar current.

Interview focus: Level Shifter

"Why can't you directly connect a 0.8V signal to a 1.2V input gate?"

Answer: A 0.8V signal may not reach the threshold voltage (Vth) of a 1.2V transistor reliably, or it may leave the PMOS and NMOS both partially on, leading to massive short-circuit power dissipation. A Level Shifter ensures clear 0/1 logic levels between domains.
