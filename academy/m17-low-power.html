Phase III: Advanced

Low Power Design

The art of efficiency. Using UPF, Clock Gating, and Multi-Voltage domains to minimize the power footprint of high-performance silicon.

1. Dynamic and Static Power reduction

Power management is now a mandatory requirement for mobile and server chips alike. We optimize for power across both the architecture (UPF) and the implementation stages.

Techniques Hierarchy:

  • Clock Gating: Disabling the clock toggle. #1 source of dynamic power savings.
  • MTCMOS (Multi-Threshold CMOS): Using a High-Vt transistor as a "Header" or "Footer" to cut off the supply from Low-Vt logic. This eliminates leakage power.
  • MSV (Multi-Supply Voltage): Lowering VDD for non-critical blocks (e.g., 0.6V for a timer) while keeping 1.0V for the main core.
  • DVFS (Dynamic Voltage & Frequency Scaling): Scaling VDD/f in real-time based on the workload (like your phone's processor).

2. UPF 2.0: Power State Tables & State Retention

The **Unified Power Format (UPF)** is a script that tells the P&R tool how the power grid should behave logically.

  • Retention Registers: Specialized flip-flops that have an "always-on" backup supply. They "save" their logic state before a power domain is switched off and "restore" it after power-on.
  • Isolation Strategy: Forcing a constant 0 or 1 at the boundary of a powered-down domain to prevent the receiving domain from detecting a "floating" or indeterminate logic level.
  • PST (Power State Table): A matrix defining which domains can be ON or OFF simultaneously. The tool uses this to automatically insert the correct level shifters and isolators.

Interview focus: Level Shifter

"Why can't you directly connect a 0.8V signal to a 1.2V input gate?"

Answer: A 0.8V signal may not reach the threshold voltage (Vth) of a 1.2V transistor reliably, or it may leave the PMOS and NMOS both partially on, leading to massive short-circuit power dissipation. A Level Shifter ensures clear 0/1 logic levels between domains.
