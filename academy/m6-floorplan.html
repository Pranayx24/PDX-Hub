Phase II: Execution

Floorplan Strategy

The chip's blueprint. Deciding die area, I/O placement, and macro positioning to minimize congestion.

1. The Foundation: Core and Die Area

Floorplanning defines the literal landscape of the silicon. Decisions made here are irreversible once implementation begins. A bad floorplan ensures timing failure or routing congestion.

Core vs Die & Aspect Ratio:

The Die Area contains the whole chip, including the pads and seal ring. The Core Area is the internal region where standard cells and macros are placed.

  • Aspect Ratio (H/W): A square chip (1.0) is easiest to route. Rectangular chips (0.5 or 2.0) are often used in mobile to fit specific form factors but create asymmetrical timing.
  • Utilization: Ratio of total cell area to the core area. Typically target 60-70%. If utilization is too low, you waste silicon; if too high, you can't route the nets.
Pin Placement Strategy

I/O pins must be placed on specific metal layers (e.g., M3 or M4) with a specific Pitch (distance between pins). Placing pins too close together creates local routing congestion (hotspots) right at the boundary.

2. Macro Placement & Congestion

Macros (RAM, ROM, Analog IPs) are large, non-standard-cell blocks. Placement rules:

  • Along Edges: Place macros along core boundaries to leave the center open for standard cell connectivity.
  • Macro Halos (Keep-outs): A placement blockage around the macro to prevent standard cells from being placed too close, ensuring space for macro-pin routing.
  • Routing Blockages: Specific regions where the tool is forbidden from routing tracks (e.g., above an analog macro to prevent noise coupling).

Interview focus: Macro Placement

"What is a Halo / Keep-out zone, and why do we need it?"

Answer: Halos are regions around macros where standard cells cannot be placed. This prevents density hotspots and ensures that macro-pin routing has dedicated tracks without interference from local standard cell pins.
