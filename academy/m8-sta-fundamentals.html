Phase II: Execution

STA Fundamentals

The gatekeeper of frequency. Static Timing Analysis (STA) ensures every path on your chip meets timing requirements.

1. Setup and Hold Timing

STA verifies that signals arrive at registers within the necessary windows around clock edges. Unlike dynamic simulation, it doesn't need vectors—it checks every path mathematically.

The Fundamental Formulas:

Setup Slack = (T_period + T_capture_clock) - (T_launch_clock + T_data_delay + T_setup_time)

Hold Slack = (T_launch_clock + T_data_delay) - (T_capture_clock + T_hold_time)

2. Timing Path Types

We analyze four primary paths in Physical Design:

  • Reg-to-Reg: Most common. Launch flip-flop to capture flip-flop. Clock period dependent.
  • In-to-Reg: Input pad to internal register. Delay must be balanced for set-up.
  • Reg-to-Out: Internal register to output pad. Delay must be balanced for hold.
  • In-to-Out: Direct combinatorial paths from input to output. Also called "Feedthroughs".

Interview focus: Clock Skew & Slack

"Can a Hold violation be fixed by decreasing the clock frequency?"

Answer: No. Since the clock period (T_period) does not appear in the hold formula, frequency has no impact on hold violations. Only adding data delay (buffers) or modifying the clock skew can fix it.
