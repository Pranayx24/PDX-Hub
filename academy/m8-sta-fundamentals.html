Phase II: Execution

STA Fundamentals

The gatekeeper of frequency. Static Timing Analysis (STA) ensures every path on your chip meets timing requirements.

1. Setup and Hold Timing

Static Timing Analysis (STA) verifies the timing of every path without needing functional simulation vectors. It evaluates the "worst-case" scenario across billions of possible paths.

The Fundamental Formulas:

Setup Slack = (T_period + T_capture_clock_min) - (T_launch_clock_max + T_data_delay_max + T_setup_time)

Hold Slack = (T_launch_clock_min + T_data_delay_min) - (T_capture_clock_max + T_hold_time)
Clock Uncertainty: Skew & Jitter

The tool subtracts a "Safety Margin" from the slack to account for random timing variations. Skew is the systematic difference in wire length; Jitter is the random temporal variation in the PLL output itself.

2. Advanced Constraints: FP & MCP

Many paths reported by the tool aren't functionally active. SDC allows us to ignore them:

  • False Path (FP): A path that logic cannot physically transition (e.g., across asynchronous clock domains). Fix: set_false_path.
  • Multicycle Path (MCP): A path where logic takes more than 1 clock cycle to resolve (e.g., in a complex divider). Fix: set_multicycle_path.
  • OCV Derates: Applying 5-10% extra delay to "late" paths and subtracting 5-10% from "early" paths to model on-chip process variation.

Interview focus: Clock Skew & Slack

"Can a Hold violation be fixed by decreasing the clock frequency?"

Answer: No. Since the clock period (T_period) does not appear in the hold formula, frequency has no impact on hold violations. Only adding data delay (buffers) or modifying the clock skew can fix it.
