Phase III: Advanced

Optimization Tech

The refinement of silicon. Using advanced techniques like Vt-swapping and gate-sizing to meet PPA targets.

1. Post-Route Optimization

Once routing is complete, the tool has precise RC (Resistance-Capacitance) values for every wire. Optimization now focuses on surgical fixes to timing and power violations without disturbing the whole layout.

Advanced PPA Levers:

  • Footprint Swapping: Replacing a cell with a different Vt variant (LVT, RVT, HVT) that occupies the exact same physical space. This fixes timing with 0 core area cost.
  • Gate Sizing: Modifying drive strength. Large cells are needed to drive long, high-RC nets but consume more dynamic power.
  • Decap Insertion: Adding decoupling capacitors to regions with high dynamic switching to maintain local voltage stability.
ULVT (Ultra Low Vt) Usage

ULVT cells are the "racecars" of the chip. They provide maximum speed but have massive leakage power. The optimizer only uses these on the top 1% of the most critical timing paths to close WNS.

2. The ECO Flow (Engineering Change Order)

At the end of the project, if a logic bug is found or a tiny timing violation remains, we don't re-run the whole P&R flow. We use an **ECO (Engineering Change Order)**.

  • Manual Patching: Modifying the netlist with TCL scripts to delete a gate or insert a buffer.
  • Eco Route: Rerouting only the nets impacted by the change while keeping everything else frozen.
  • Metal-Only ECO: A cost-saving ECO where only the top metal layers are changed, reused existing base layer silicon masks (much cheaper for foundry).

Interview focus: Vt Selection

"What is the disadvantage of using only Low-Vt (LVT) cells in your design?"

Answer: LVT cells have a very thin gate oxide, leading to high sub-threshold leakage current. A design using only LVT will exceed power specifications even when data is not switching.
