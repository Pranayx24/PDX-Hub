Phase II: Execution

Routing Phase

The final interconnect. Creating millions of metal paths to connect logic Gates without DRC violations.

1. Global and Detailed Routing

Routing occurs after CTS is finalized. The tool uses a grid-based Manhattan approach (vertical vs horizontal metal layers) to resolve billions of electrical net paths.

The Grid Concept:

The core is divided into G-Cells (Gridges). Tracks are the literal "railway lines" that metal must follow.

  • Global Routing (GR): Assigns each net to a series of G-Cells to check if wire resources are available.
  • Track Assignment: Binds each net to a literal metal track (X/Y coordinate).
  • Detail Routing (DR): Resolves local violations (spacing, width, min-area) by performing surgery at the track level.

2. Signal Integrity & Reliability

At 7nm and below, wires are so close that they behave like capacitors, leading to Crosstalk.

  • SI-Crosstalk: When an aggressor net switches, it induces a glitch on a victim net, changing its delay or even flipping its state.
  • Redundant Vias (Double Vias): Detail routing prioritizes adding two vias per connection. If one via fails due to manufacturing defects, the net survives.
  • Antenna Diodes: Inserting a reverse-biased diode close to the input pin of a gate to drain static charge accumulated during the plasma etching of metal.

Interview focus: Routing Congestion

"What is a G-Cell, and how does the tool use it for routing?"

Answer: G-Cells (Global Cells) are virtual buckets the tool uses to estimate routing density across the chip. By checking the number of nets entering/exiting each G-Cell, the tool can identify congestion hotspots during global routing.
